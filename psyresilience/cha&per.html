Challenge

   Developing the resilience of our minds is a long process in which we must persevere to succeed. According to the research of the psychologist Salvatore Maddi (2006), the first essential element for mental resilience is challenge. He points out that resilient people are those who “see difficulty as a challenge, instead of a paralyzing event” (2006).
   From time to time, we let ourselves be controlled by the situations we face in our lives, and we cannot move forward to where we want to go. However, creating this resilience means that we are practicing our minds to think differently than what we were used to. This view of difficult situations as a challenge means that we are learning from our mistakes and looking at the problem from different angles to try to solve it instead of just giving up (2006).
   Are we still allowed to think that something is hard? The answer is a loud YES! We are allowed to feel our emotions. In fact, acknowledging our emotions and feeling them is good for our mental health. Therefore, what we are learning is that we can feel our emotions, but that we have to change the way we view difficult situations because as we see them as challenges that we can overcome, we will stop blaming ourselves for not being able to accomplish them.

Commitment

   Commitment is the second essential element for a resilient mind. Resilient minds are those which are committed to their lives. Committing is not just restricted to work or school, but it also means committing to relationships with family and friends, to causes that one cares about, to religion and spiritual beliefs (Maddi, 2006).
   Committing ourselves to the life around us will give us a reason to wake up in the morning, helping our minds to become stronger because we will feel that we have a purpose. Feeling that you have a purpose is one of the things that people who face mental challenges most often struggle with. They often feel that there is nothing left to do. That is why committing to ourselves, to our lives, and to those around us is a key point to develop this resilience.
   By committing, we help ourselves to find purpose and perseverance. It is a lot easier to fight for something when you know what and why you are fighting for (2006). Therefore, there will be an increase on success for someone as they keep fighting against any illnesses if they understand what they are fighting against and the reasons why they are fighting for. It is in this moment that the person fighting needs to be surrounded by those who love him or her, because they will have at least one reason why they are fighting, and that they are not fighting alone.
